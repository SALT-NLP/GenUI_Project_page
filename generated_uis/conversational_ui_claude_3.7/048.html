Fundamental Principles of Quantum Physics

Quantum Nature of Energy

Energy is not continuous but comes in discrete packets called quanta. This was first proposed by Max Planck in 1900 to explain blackbody radiation. The energy of a quantum is proportional to its frequency: E = hf, where h is Planck's constant.

Wave-Particle Duality

All particles exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties. This means electrons, photons, and even larger particles can behave as waves under certain conditions and as particles under others. This was demonstrated by the famous double-slit experiment.

Quantum Superposition

Quantum systems can exist in multiple states simultaneously until measured. This is mathematically represented by the wave function, which gives probability amplitudes for different possible states.

Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

It's impossible to simultaneously know both the position and momentum of a particle with perfect precision. The more precisely one property is measured, the less precisely the other can be known: ΔxΔp ≥ ħ/2, where ħ is the reduced Planck constant.

Quantum Entanglement

When particles become entangled, their quantum states become correlated in such a way that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently of the others, regardless of distance separating them.

Quantum Tunneling

Particles can penetrate energy barriers that would be impossible according to classical physics. This is a direct consequence of their wave-like nature and the probabilistic interpretation of quantum mechanics.

Quantum Spin

Elementary particles have an intrinsic property called spin, which is quantized and can only take specific values. Unlike classical spinning objects, quantum spin is an inherent property not related to actual rotation.

Quantum Field Theory

In modern quantum physics, particles are viewed as excitations of underlying fields that permeate all of spacetime. This framework unifies quantum mechanics with special relativity.
