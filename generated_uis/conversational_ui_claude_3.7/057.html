I'd be happy to help you approach this complex environmental monitoring dataset. This is exactly the kind of challenge where a structured methodology can make a significant difference. Here's how I would tackle it:

Data Cleaning Approach

First, you'll need to handle the data gaps and inconsistencies:

  1. Identify and document missing data patterns: Look for temporal patterns (specific time periods), spatial patterns (particular neighborhoods), or sensor-specific issues.

  2. Normalize measurement units: Ensure consistency across all sensors and time periods.

  3. Handle outliers: Use statistical methods like z-scores or IQR to identify anomalous readings that might represent sensor malfunctions versus actual pollution events.

  4. Imputation strategies: For missing data, consider:

    • Simple methods (mean/median) for random gaps
    • Time-series specific methods (interpolation, LOCF) for temporal data
    • More sophisticated approaches like multiple imputation for systematic gaps

Analysis Framework

Once your data is cleaned:

  1. Exploratory data analysis: Start with univariate analysis of each variable, then move to bivariate relationships between air quality and potential contributors.

  2. Temporal analysis: Examine daily, weekly, and seasonal patterns in air quality.

  3. Spatial analysis: Map pollution hotspots and overlay with traffic, construction, and other variables.

  4. Statistical modeling:

    • Consider mixed-effects models to account for neighborhood clustering
    • Use time-series analysis to identify trends and seasonality
    • Implement causal inference methods to isolate the effect of specific contributors

  5. Policy intervention modeling: Create counterfactual scenarios based on proposed interventions to estimate potential impacts.

Visualization Strategy

For effective communication of your findings:

  1. Interactive dashboards: Create layered visualizations that allow stakeholders to explore relationships at different temporal and spatial scales.

  2. Geospatial mapping: Use choropleth or heat maps to visualize pollution distribution across neighborhoods.

  3. Comparative visualizations: Show before/after scenarios for policy interventions.

  4. Uncertainty visualization: Incorporate confidence intervals or ensemble forecasts to communicate the reliability of your predictions.
