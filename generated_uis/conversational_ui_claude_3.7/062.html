Your research approach combining GIS spatial analysis, public health data, qualitative interviews, and participatory mapping across different socioeconomic neighborhoods is quite comprehensive and well-suited for addressing environmental justice questions.

For interdisciplinary research like yours that integrates multiple data types while foregrounding justice concerns, I recommend considering the following methodological frameworks:

  1. Convergent Parallel Mixed Methods Design with an Environmental Justice Lens

    This approach allows you to collect and analyze both quantitative (GIS, health data) and qualitative (interviews, participatory mapping) data simultaneously, then integrate findings during interpretation. The environmental justice lens ensures equity considerations remain central throughout.

  2. Community-Based Participatory Research (CBPR) Framework

    CBPR would be particularly strong for your study as it emphasizes community engagement throughout the research process, recognizes power dynamics, and ensures findings directly benefit affected communities - all critical for environmental justice work.

  3. Socio-Ecological Systems Framework

    This framework would help you analyze the complex relationships between environmental, social, economic, and governance factors affecting green space access and outcomes across different communities.

To meaningfully integrate your diverse data types:

  • Use triangulation strategies to cross-validate findings across methods
  • Develop a shared conceptual framework that links spatial access metrics to well-being indicators
  • Consider sequential design elements where initial GIS analysis informs participant selection for qualitative components
  • Create integrated visualizations that combine spatial data with qualitative insights
  • Plan for community verification of integrated findings
