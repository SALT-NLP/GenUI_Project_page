I'd be happy to explain the differences between formal and casual Japanese. These are important distinctions in the language that affect vocabulary, grammar, sentence structure, and social context.

In Japanese, the level of formality is more explicitly built into the language system than in English. Here are the key differences:

Honorific System

Formal Japanese involves an extensive honorific system called keigo (敬語), which has three main categories:

  • Teineigo (丁寧語) - polite language
  • Sonkeigo (尊敬語) - respectful language for elevating others
  • Kenjougo (謙譲語) - humble language for lowering oneself

Casual Japanese (kudaketa nihongo) omits these honorific forms.

Verb Endings

One of the most noticeable differences is in verb endings:

  • Formal: Uses the "-masu" form (食べます/tabemasu - "eat")
  • Casual: Uses plain form (食べる/taberu - "eat")

Sentence Endings

  • Formal: Sentences often end with "desu" (です) and "masu" (ます)
  • Casual: Uses plain forms and can drop sentence-final particles

Vocabulary Choices

Many words have formal and casual variants:

  • Formal: わたくし (watakushi) for "I"
  • Casual: わたし (watashi) or casual pronouns like ぼく (boku) or おれ (ore)

Particles and Contractions

  • Formal: Uses all grammatical particles consistently
  • Casual: Often drops particles and uses contractions (じゃない instead of ではない)

Social Context

Formal Japanese is used in:

  • Business settings
  • When speaking to superiors or elders
  • Public speaking
  • First meetings with strangers

Casual Japanese is used with:

  • Friends and family
  • Peers of equal social standing
  • In relaxed social settings
