That's a fantastic initiative — precision farming can significantly enhance yield, reduce waste, and promote sustainable agriculture. Since your team brings strong agricultural expertise, and you’ve collected rich data sources (multispectral imagery, soil sensors, weather, and yield), you're well-positioned to benefit from data-driven methods. Here’s a high-level roadmap you can follow, tailored to your team's context:

Step 1: Organize and Preprocess Your Data

Objective: Make the data usable for modeling and visualization.

  • Consolidate by Location & Time:

    • Match drone imagery, sensor readings, and yield data by GPS coordinates and timestamps.
    • Divide your fields into uniform grid cells or management zones (e.g., 10x10 meters).

  • Clean & Normalize:

    • Handle missing sensor readings and cloudy drone images.
    • Normalize data ranges (e.g., NDVI values between 0–1).

  • Extract Features:

    • From imagery: compute vegetation indices (NDVI, EVI, GNDVI).
    • From soil data: moisture, pH, nutrients (NPK), temperature.
    • From weather: rainfall, temperature, humidity over time windows.
    • From past yields: interpolate historical productivity by section.

Step 2: Build a Crop Stress Detection System

Objective: Identify early signs of crop stress using historical patterns.

  • Labeling Strategy (if manual inspection is possible):

    • Use your team’s expertise to label regions with known crop stress signs in past seasons.
    • Alternatively, define "stress" by drop in vegetation index over time or below-yield performance.

  • Model Choices:

    • Start with classical models like Random Forest or XGBoost (interpretable and accurate).
    • Inputs: vegetation indices, soil moisture, temperature, recent rainfall.
    • Output: binary or multi-class label (no stress / water stress / nutrient stress / pest stress).

  • Visualization:

    • Generate field maps color-coded by predicted stress level.

Step 3: Predict Yield by Field Section

Objective: Estimate future yield before harvest, by area.

  • Approach:

    • Use regression models to predict yield based on mid-season multispectral indices, soil properties, and weather.
    • Inputs: aggregated features by field section.
    • Outputs: yield per hectare.

  • Models: Try regression models (Linear Regression, XGBoost, or LSTM if doing time-series analysis).

Step 4: Recommend Targeted Interventions

Objective: Link identified stress or low predicted yield to action.

  • Decision Rules (initial approach):

    • If soil moisture < threshold → recommend irrigation.
    • If NDVI is low + N-deficiency → recommend nitrogen application.

  • Advanced Option:

    • Use historical intervention and yield data to train a prescriptive model or simulate outcomes of actions.

Step 5: Build a Simple Dashboard

Objective: Present findings to your team clearly.

  • Tools: Start with something accessible:

    • QGIS or Google Earth Engine for spatial visualization.
    • Streamlit or Power BI for dashboards.

  • Include:

    • Stress heatmaps.
    • Yield prediction charts.
    • Recommended action layers.
    • Trends over time.

Final Tips

  • Software Stack: Python + libraries like Pandas, Scikit-learn, Rasterio, and OpenCV for image handling.
  • Model Training Help: Consider partnering with a local university or hiring a data science consultant part-time.
  • Don't Overcomplicate: Even basic rules-based systems, if well-calibrated, can offer big gains.
