That sounds like a fascinating and complex project! Integrating and analyzing this kind of interdisciplinary data is both a challenge and an opportunity to uncover impactful insights that can guide future urban planning. Here’s how you might approach this multifaceted task:

1. Data Integration and Cleaning

First, ensure that your data from different sources is harmonized. This involves:

  • Standardizing variables (e.g., health metrics across cities might be recorded differently, so you may need to create a consistent framework for comparison).
  • Handling missing data using imputation or other techniques, especially considering that some datasets (e.g., surveys or environmental sensors) may have gaps.
  • Geospatial alignment: Ensure that your data, particularly from environmental sensors or property values, can be mapped to geographic boundaries (like city districts or neighborhoods) to make the analysis spatially coherent.

2. Addressing Confounding Variables

Since your study spans different cities, controlling for confounders is critical:

  • Multilevel models: These can help account for nested data (e.g., health data within neighborhoods within cities). Multilevel models would allow you to adjust for city-level factors that might confound individual-level health outcomes.
  • Propensity score matching: This can help adjust for selection bias in cities that might have more green space interventions due to pre-existing health or economic characteristics.
  • Instrumental variable analysis: In case there’s endogeneity (e.g., areas with better health outcomes might be more likely to receive green space interventions), you could use instrumental variables to get a clearer causal interpretation.

3. Statistical Methods to Identify Patterns

Once your data is cleaned and aligned, you'll need to conduct robust statistical analyses to identify meaningful relationships:

  • Difference-in-differences (DiD): This is useful for pre/post comparisons when you have data from cities with and without green space interventions. DiD will help account for time-varying confounders and allow you to estimate the effect of the intervention on health outcomes.
  • Spatial econometrics: If you have data at a spatial level (e.g., neighborhood, district), spatial regressions can capture spatial autocorrelation—i.e., the possibility that health outcomes or environmental variables are correlated with those in neighboring areas.
  • Longitudinal analysis: If you have data over time (e.g., before and after the intervention), techniques like mixed-effects models can help analyze time series data while controlling for random effects at different levels (individual, neighborhood, city).
  • Machine learning techniques: For identifying non-linear relationships or complex interactions (e.g., between environmental factors and demographic characteristics), machine learning methods like random forests, support vector machines, or neural networks could be used. However, interpretability may be a challenge, so using explainable AI techniques (e.g., SHAP values) could be useful.

4. Visualization for Academic and Policy Use

  • Interactive Dashboards: Tools like Tableau or Power BI could allow for interactive exploration of the data. You could visualize how different variables (e.g., green space coverage, air quality, socio-economic indicators) correlate with health outcomes, and give stakeholders a platform to test different scenarios.
  • Geospatial Visualizations: Use GIS software (e.g., ArcGIS, QGIS, or even R’s leaflet package) to map out the spatial distribution of green spaces, health metrics, and demographic factors. This can help visually communicate disparities in access to green spaces and their impact on community health.
  • Multivariate Plots: Use scatterplot matrices, heatmaps, or pair plots to display relationships between key variables, such as income, green space coverage, air quality, and health outcomes.
  • Before-and-After Maps: These could highlight changes in health metrics or green space access post-intervention, which could be compelling for policymakers.
  • Time Series Plots: If you have data over time, consider plotting changes in health outcomes alongside key environmental or policy changes to highlight trends.

5. Modeling Causal Inferences

  • Causal impact analysis (e.g., via causal graphs or structural equation modeling) can help explore causal relationships between environmental interventions and health outcomes while accounting for confounders.
  • Path Analysis or SEM: These methods are useful for understanding how different variables (e.g., social, environmental, and economic factors) interact to influence health outcomes, which can help pinpoint direct and indirect effects of green space interventions.

6. Recommendations for Policy

  • Scenario Modeling: You can develop simulations to forecast potential health outcomes based on different urban planning scenarios, such as increasing or decreasing green space. This can help policymakers visualize potential outcomes of their decisions.
  • Equity Analysis: Consider breaking down health outcomes by demographic groups (e.g., income, race, age) to identify whether certain populations are disproportionately benefiting or losing from green space interventions.
  • Cost-Benefit Analysis: By integrating economic data with health outcomes, you can provide policymakers with a clearer picture of the economic value of green space in terms of public health savings (e.g., fewer hospital visits, reduced healthcare costs).

7. Collaboration and Feedback

Since your work spans multiple disciplines (urban planning, public health, economics), ensure continuous collaboration with experts in each field. This will help refine your analysis and make sure the findings are relevant and actionable across disciplines. Regular feedback loops with stakeholders (e.g., local governments, health departments) can ensure that your findings are aligned with real-world needs.

By using a combination of robust statistical methods, careful data integration, and clear visualizations, you'll be able to tell a compelling story about the impacts of green space on health that can drive both academic and policy advancements.
