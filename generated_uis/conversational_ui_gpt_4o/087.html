You're tackling a mixed-methods research project, which combines both quantitative (your survey) and qualitative (your interviews) data. To integrate these effectively and maintain academic rigor in your analysis chapter, consider the following methodological approach:

Recommended Approach: Convergent Parallel Mixed Methods Design

This is one of the most widely accepted mixed-methods approaches for integrating quantitative and qualitative data.

Key Features:

  • Concurrent data collection and analysis: You've already done this by collecting surveys and interviews during the same timeframe.
  • Separate analysis, then integration: Analyze quantitative and qualitative data independently, then compare and synthesize the findings in your analysis chapter.
  • Equal priority: Both datasets are treated as equally important.

Steps to Implement:

  1. Quantitative Analysis:

    • Use descriptive and inferential statistics to identify trends, patterns, and correlations (e.g., between social media use and self-reported anxiety or sleep quality).
    • Present this in charts, tables, and statistical summaries.

  2. Qualitative Analysis:

    • Code your interview transcripts using thematic analysis (e.g., Braun & Clarke method).
    • Identify recurring themes like peer pressure, online validation, or FOMO (fear of missing out).

  3. Integration (Crucial Part):

    • Compare results side-by-side: Identify areas where the qualitative themes explain, contradict, or add nuance to the quantitative results.
      • Example: If the survey shows a strong correlation between screen time and anxiety, use interview excerpts to explore how teens describe the causes of that anxiety.
    • Use a joint display table (a visual or tabular format) to align quantitative findings with qualitative themes.
      • Columns might include: Quantitative Result, Qualitative Theme, Interpretation/Synthesis.

  4. Maintain Academic Rigor:

    • Justify your methodological choices with references (e.g., Creswell & Plano Clark, 2018).
    • Use triangulation to enhance validity—show how different data types converge on similar conclusions.
    • Be transparent about discrepancies—if the data don’t align, explore possible reasons (e.g., different interpretations of terms, limitations of self-report).

Example Structure for Your Analysis Chapter:

  1. Introduction to the integration method
  2. Quantitative results
  3. Qualitative themes
  4. Integrated analysis (where you bring them together)
  5. Interpretation and discussion
