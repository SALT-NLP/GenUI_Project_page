That sounds like an interesting and important study! I'd be happy to help you organize your data and identify meaningful patterns. Here's a step-by-step approach we can take to work through your analysis and present your findings effectively:

1. Data Organization

Start by ensuring your data is well-organized in a format that's easy to analyze. You can use software like Excel, Google Sheets, or statistical tools like R or SPSS. Ideally, you should have columns for:

  • Participant ID (to keep track of each individual)
  • Sleep Duration (e.g., average hours per night)
  • Sleep Quality Metrics (e.g., a score or rating based on sleep quality surveys)
  • Class Attendance (percentage or count of classes attended)
  • Test Scores (could be exam scores, overall GPA, or specific subject scores)

2. Initial Data Exploration

Before diving into complex analyses, take a look at the basic descriptive statistics (mean, median, standard deviation) for each of your variables. This will give you a feel for your data and help identify any obvious trends or outliers.

For example:

  • Average sleep duration: How much sleep do students typically get?
  • Average class attendance: What percentage of classes are attended by students?
  • Average test scores: How well are students performing academically?

This can be done with basic tools like Excel or by using statistical software.

3. Correlation Analysis

To identify relationships between sleep patterns and academic performance, the first thing to do is check for correlations. This can help you identify which variables move together.

  • Sleep Duration vs. Academic Performance: Does the amount of sleep correlate with test scores or class attendance?
  • Sleep Quality vs. Academic Performance: How does the quality of sleep relate to performance?
  • Class Attendance vs. Test Scores: Is there a relationship between how often students attend class and their academic performance?

In Excel, you can use the CORREL() function to compute Pearson’s correlation coefficient, which will give you a value between -1 and 1. A value closer to 1 means a strong positive correlation, and a value closer to -1 means a strong negative correlation.

4. Regression Analysis

Once you’ve identified potential correlations, you may want to move to regression analysis to quantify the relationships between variables. For example:

  • Multiple Regression: This can help you understand how sleep duration, sleep quality, and class attendance collectively impact academic performance. You can set academic performance (e.g., test scores) as the dependent variable and the others as independent variables.

This can be done with tools like Excel or statistical software like R, SPSS, or Python.

5. Visualization

Visualization is key for presenting your findings clearly:

  • Scatter Plots: Visualize the relationships between two continuous variables, like sleep duration vs. test scores.
  • Box Plots: Show the distribution of test scores across different levels of sleep quality.
  • Bar Charts: Compare class attendance rates among different groups (e.g., students who sleep more vs. those who sleep less).

Use software like Excel, Tableau, or even R’s ggplot2 to create these visuals.

6. Statistical Significance

Make sure to assess the statistical significance of your results, typically using p-values. For example, in regression analysis, you can check whether the relationship between sleep and academic performance is statistically significant (e.g., p < 0.05).

7. Interpreting the Results

After performing the analyses, you can interpret the findings:

  • What do the correlations tell you? For instance, a positive correlation between sleep duration and test scores would suggest that students who sleep more tend to perform better academically.
  • Does sleep quality matter more than duration? If sleep quality is a stronger predictor of performance than sleep duration, this could be an important finding for your dissertation.
  • Is attendance important? If class attendance is a significant predictor of academic performance, you can highlight this in your recommendations.

8. Presenting Your Findings

When writing your dissertation:

  • Introduction: Explain the context and importance of your study.
  • Methodology: Describe how you collected data and the tools used for analysis.
  • Results: Present your descriptive statistics, correlation results, regression analysis, and visualizations.
  • Discussion: Interpret the results, relate them to existing literature, and suggest implications (e.g., how improving sleep quality or attendance could benefit students’ academic performance).
