What's the difference between formal and casual Japanese?

The difference between formal and casual Japanese primarily lies in the language's level of politeness, which affects verb conjugation, vocabulary, and overall tone. Here's a breakdown of the key differences:

1. Verb Conjugation

  • Formal: When speaking formally, Japanese speakers use the -masu form for verbs. For example:
    • 食べる (taberu, "to eat") becomes 食べます (tabemasu) in formal speech.
  • Casual: In casual conversation, the plain form (or dictionary form) is used. For example:
    • 食べる (taberu) remains as is in casual speech.
    • Another example: 行く (iku, "to go") becomes 行きます (ikimasu) in formal speech, but stays as 行く (iku) in casual speech.

2. Pronouns

  • Formal: Certain pronouns like 私 (watashi, "I") are often used in formal settings, as they sound more neutral and polite.
  • Casual: Pronouns may be dropped more often, especially in informal conversations, since the subject is often implied. When used, informal pronouns like 俺 (ore, "I" for males) or 僕 (boku, "I" for males) are common in casual settings.

3. Honorifics and Titles

  • Formal: Honorifics such as -さん (san), -さま (sama), and -先生 (sensei) are frequently used to show respect. For example, 田中さん (Tanaka-san) or 鈴木先生 (Suzuki-sensei).
  • Casual: In informal settings, people may use just the person’s name without an honorific or use more familiar or affectionate forms like -ちゃん (chan) or -くん (kun) for friends or younger people. For example, 田中 (Tanaka) or 鈴木くん (Suzuki-kun).

4. Sentence Endings

  • Formal: The way a sentence ends can indicate its formality. In formal speech, you might use です (desu) or ます (masu) to soften or elevate the tone. For example:
    • 行きます (ikimasu, "I will go") is formal.
  • Casual: In casual speech, sentence-ending particles and forms like だ (da) or よ (yo) might be used. For example:
    • 行く (iku, "I'm going") is casual, with the form “だ” being implied.

5. Vocabulary

  • Formal: In formal situations, more sophisticated or respectful words are used. For example:
    • 食べる (taberu, "to eat") becomes 召し上がる (meshiagaru) in a very formal or respectful context.
  • Casual: Informal vocabulary might be more straightforward, using words like 食う (kuu, "to eat") for a rougher or more casual tone.

6. Context and Usage

  • Formal: Used in situations where respect is important, such as in business, with strangers, or in public speeches. It reflects a more distant relationship.
  • Casual: Used with close friends, family, or peers in informal settings, where there’s little need for distance or formality.

7. Politeness Forms

  • Keigo (敬語): This is the honorific or respectful language that goes beyond basic formality, including humble forms (for yourself) and respectful forms (for others). It’s used in more official contexts, like talking to superiors or customers.
    • Example (keigo): おっしゃる (ossharu, "to say" - respectful form of 言う, iu).
    • Casual form: 言う (iu) in a casual conversation.

Example Sentences:

  • Formal:

    • 今日はお元気ですか？ (Kyou wa ogenki desu ka?) – "How are you today?"
    • 食べますか？ (Tabemasu ka?) – "Would you like to eat?"

  • Casual:

    • 今日は元気？ (Kyou wa genki?) – "How are you today?"
    • 食べる？ (Taberu?) – "Wanna eat?"

In summary, formal Japanese is used in respectful, professional, or unfamiliar contexts, and it often involves more structured speech with respectful verbs, titles, and endings. Casual Japanese is used in informal situations with friends or close acquaintances and typically uses simpler sentence structures and less honorific language.
