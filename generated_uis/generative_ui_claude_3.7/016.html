Question 3

Which of the following is a contraindication for prescribing Lisinopril?

Select the most appropriate answer based on current guidelines.

History of diabetes
Patients with diabetes often benefit from ACE inhibitors due to their renoprotective effects.
History of angioedema related to ACE inhibitors
Previous angioedema with ACE inhibitors is a significant contraindication due to risk of recurrence.
Hypertension
Hypertension is a primary indication for Lisinopril therapy, not a contraindication.
Heart failure
Heart failure is often treated with ACE inhibitors like Lisinopril to improve outcomes.
check_circle

Correct!

A history of angioedema related to ACE inhibitors is an absolute contraindication for Lisinopril. Recurrent angioedema can be life-threatening and occurs in up to 35% of patients who try another ACE inhibitor after experiencing angioedema.

3. New Anticoagulant Protocols

Recent studies have demonstrated improved outcomes with adjusted dosing protocols for direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) in specific patient populations. The 2023 guidelines now recommend more nuanced approaches to dosing based on patient characteristics including age, weight, renal function, and concomitant medications.

For patients with atrial fibrillation and moderate renal impairment (CrCl 30-50 mL/min), dose adjustments are now recommended as follows:

  • Apixaban: 2.5mg twice daily if patient meets at least two of the following criteria: age ≥80 years, weight ≤60kg, or serum creatinine ≥1.5mg/dL
  • Rivaroxaban: 15mg once daily with the evening meal
  • Dabigatran: 110mg twice daily for patients ≥75 years or with high bleeding risk
  • Edoxaban: 30mg once daily

quiz Knowledge Check

For a 78-year-old patient weighing 58kg with atrial fibrillation and a CrCl of 45 mL/min, which of the following would be the most appropriate apixaban dosing?

5mg twice daily
2.5mg twice daily
10mg once daily
1.25mg twice daily

Additionally, the guidelines now emphasize the importance of regular monitoring of renal function in patients on DOACs, with recommended testing frequency based on baseline renal function:

  • CrCl ≥60 mL/min: Annually
  • CrCl 30-59 mL/min: Every 6 months
  • CrCl 15-29 mL/min: Every 3 months
check_circle

Knowledge Check Correct!

The correct answer is 2.5mg twice daily. This patient meets two criteria for dose reduction: age ≥75 years and weight ≤60kg. According to the 2023 guidelines, patients meeting at least two of these criteria should receive the reduced dose of apixaban to minimize bleeding risk while maintaining efficacy.

Clinical Practice Scenario

Anticoagulation Management Scenario

This scenario will test your knowledge of the updated anticoagulation protocols in a realistic clinical setting. Your decisions will affect patient outcomes.

Patient Case

Mrs. Eleanor Smith is a 76-year-old woman with a history of paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, hypertension, and type 2 diabetes. She was recently diagnosed with a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in her left leg following a long flight. Her current medications include metformin, lisinopril, and metoprolol. Her laboratory results show:

  • Weight: 62 kg
  • Serum creatinine: 1.3 mg/dL
  • Calculated CrCl: 42 mL/min
  • Hemoglobin: 11.8 g/dL
  • Platelets: 180,000/μL

She has no known drug allergies but experienced a minor GI bleed 5 years ago while taking aspirin for primary prevention.

Decision Point 1: Initial Anticoagulation Therapy

What would be the most appropriate initial anticoagulation therapy for Mrs. Smith?

Option A: Warfarin with initial LMWH bridge, target INR 2-3
Option B: Rivaroxaban 15 mg twice daily for 21 days, followed by 20 mg once daily
Option C: Rivaroxaban 15 mg once daily with evening meal
Option D: Apixaban 10 mg twice daily for 7 days, followed by 5 mg twice daily

Clinical Reasoning

Option C (Rivaroxaban 15 mg once daily) is the most appropriate choice for this patient. According to the 2023 guidelines, patients with DVT and moderate renal impairment (CrCl 30-50 mL/min) should receive a reduced dose of rivaroxaban at 15 mg once daily with the evening meal.

Option B would be an overdose given her renal function. Option A (warfarin) is a reasonable alternative but requires more frequent monitoring and has more drug-food interactions, making DOACs preferred when appropriate. Option D represents an overdose of apixaban given her age, weight, and renal function.
