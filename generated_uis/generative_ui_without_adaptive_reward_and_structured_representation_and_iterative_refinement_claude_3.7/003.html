Literature Review Methods

  • Systematic Review
  • Meta-Analysis
  • Narrative Review
  • Scoping Review
  • Integrative Review

Systematic Review

A systematic review is a type of literature review that collects and critically analyzes multiple research studies or papers. It is designed to provide a comprehensive summary of current literature relevant to a specific research question. Systematic reviews follow a rigorous, predefined methodology to minimize bias and ensure reproducibility.

  • Define a clear research question.
  • Develop a search strategy across multiple databases.
  • Screen and select studies based on inclusion/exclusion criteria.
  • Extract data from included studies.
  • Critically appraise the quality of included studies.
  • Synthesize findings (qualitatively or quantitatively).
  • Report findings transparently.

Pros: High level of evidence, minimizes bias, reproducible, provides a comprehensive overview.

Cons: Time-consuming, resource-intensive, requires specialized skills, potential for publication bias.

Best used when you need to answer a specific, focused question based on all available evidence, often in healthcare or social sciences.

Meta-Analysis

A meta-analysis is a statistical technique that combines the results of multiple studies included in a systematic review. It aims to provide a single estimate of the effect size for a particular intervention or association, increasing statistical power and precision.

  • Perform a systematic review to identify relevant studies.
  • Extract quantitative data (effect sizes, standard errors).
  • Assess heterogeneity among studies.
  • Choose an appropriate statistical model (fixed-effect or random-effects).
  • Calculate the pooled effect size.
  • Conduct sensitivity and subgroup analyses (if applicable).
  • Interpret and report findings, including limitations.

Pros: Increases statistical power, provides a more precise estimate of effect, can resolve conflicting findings from individual studies.

Cons: Requires homogeneous studies (or careful handling of heterogeneity), "garbage in, garbage out" principle applies, requires statistical expertise.

Used in conjunction with a systematic review when there are multiple quantitative studies addressing the same question and you want to statistically synthesize their results.

Narrative Review

A narrative review (also known as a traditional review) provides a broad overview of a topic based on existing literature. It is often used to provide context, identify key themes, or highlight gaps in research. Unlike systematic reviews, narrative reviews do not typically follow a strict protocol for searching or selecting studies and are more subjective.

  • Identify a broad topic or question.
  • Search for relevant literature (less structured than systematic).
  • Read and synthesize key findings and themes.
  • Structure the review logically (e.g., by theme, chronology).
  • Write a narrative summary of the literature.

Pros: Flexible, can cover a wide range of literature, useful for introducing a topic or identifying broad trends, less time-consuming.

Cons: Prone to bias (selection, reporting), not reproducible, findings are subjective summaries rather than rigorous synthesis.

Suitable for providing background information, exploring theoretical concepts, or identifying areas for future research when a comprehensive, evidence-based answer to a specific question is not the primary goal.

Scoping Review

A scoping review aims to map the existing literature on a broad topic or question, identifying key concepts, types of evidence, and gaps in research. It is useful for exploring the breadth and depth of a research area rather than synthesizing findings to answer a specific question.

  • Identify the research question (often broad).
  • Develop a search strategy.
  • Screen and select studies.
  • Extract data relevant to the scope (e.g., study design, population, concepts).
  • Collating, summarizing, and reporting the results.
  • Consultation (optional, but recommended).

Pros: Maps a broad field, identifies evidence types and gaps, useful for informing future research or policy, can include diverse study designs.

Cons: Does not typically assess study quality or synthesize findings in detail, can be time-consuming for very broad topics.

Use when you want to understand the nature and extent of research on a topic, identify key concepts, or determine if a systematic review is feasible.

Integrative Review

An integrative review synthesizes findings from diverse methodologies (e.g., quantitative, qualitative, mixed methods) to develop a more comprehensive understanding of a phenomenon. It can include theoretical literature as well as empirical studies.

  • Formulate the problem.
  • Search the literature.
  • Evaluate data from included studies.
  • Analyze data.
  • Present the results.

Pros: Can provide a holistic view by including varied evidence, useful for complex phenomena, can integrate theory and research.

Cons: Can be challenging to synthesize diverse methodologies, potential for bias if not conducted rigorously, less standardized than systematic reviews.

Appropriate when the research question requires synthesizing findings from different types of studies or integrating theoretical and empirical literature.
