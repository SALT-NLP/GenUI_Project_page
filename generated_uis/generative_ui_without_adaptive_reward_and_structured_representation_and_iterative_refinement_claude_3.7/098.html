Formal vs. Casual Japanese

Understanding Keigo (敬語) and Casual Japanese

Japanese has distinct levels of politeness, broadly categorized as Formal (often using Keigo, 敬語) and Casual. Choosing the appropriate level depends heavily on the social context, including your relationship with the listener, their social status relative to yours, and the setting (e.g., work, school, home).

Mastering the difference is crucial for effective communication and showing respect in Japanese society.

Formal Japanese (丁寧語 - Teineigo & 敬語 - Keigo)

Formal Japanese, or Teineigo (polite language), is used in most standard interactions with people you don't know well, superiors, or in formal settings. Keigo is a broader term that includes Teineigo, as well as more complex honorific (Sonkeigo) and humble (Kenjougo) forms used when speaking about or to superiors and about oneself or one's in-group to outsiders.

Key characteristics include:

  • Using ます (-masu) and です (-desu) endings for verbs and adjectives/nouns.
  • Using polite prefixes like お (o-) and ご (go-).
  • Employing specific vocabulary and verb forms unique to Keigo.

Casual Japanese (普通体 - Futsūtai)

Casual Japanese, or Futsūtai (plain form), is used among close friends, family members, or when speaking to people considered lower in status or age. It's also the form typically used in casual writing, diaries, or when quoting someone.

Key characteristics include:

  • Using plain verb forms (dictionary form, -ta form, -nai form, etc.).
  • Using plain adjective and noun forms (without です).
  • Using casual particles like ね (ne), よ (yo), and ぞ (zo).
  • Using shortened or colloquial vocabulary.

Key Differences in Action

Here are some examples illustrating the difference between formal and casual Japanese across various sentence structures.

Formal:

行きます。

I will go. (Polite)

Casual:

行くよ。

I'm going! (Casual)

Formal:

これは本です。

This is a book. (Polite)

Casual:

これ、本だね。

This is a book, huh? (Casual)

Formal:

昨日、映画を見ました。

I watched a movie yesterday. (Polite)

Casual:

昨日、映画見たよ。

Watched a movie yesterday! (Casual)

Formal:

お名前は何ですか？

What is your name? (Polite)

Casual:

名前なんて言うの？

What's your name? (Casual)

Tip: When in doubt, it's generally safer to use formal Japanese. Using casual Japanese inappropriately can be considered rude.
