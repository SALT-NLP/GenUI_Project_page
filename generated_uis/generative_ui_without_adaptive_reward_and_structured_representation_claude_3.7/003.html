Systematic Review

A systematic review is a rigorous and transparent method of synthesizing existing research evidence. It aims to answer a specific research question by identifying, selecting, critically appraising, and synthesizing relevant studies.

  • Formulate a clear research question (often using PICO frameworkPICO: Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome).
  • Develop a comprehensive search strategy across multiple databases.
  • Screen search results based on predefined inclusion/exclusion criteria.
  • Extract data from eligible studies.
  • Critically appraise the quality and risk of bias of included studies.
  • Synthesize findings (qualitatively or quantitatively).
  • Report findings transparently.

Pros: Highly reliable and reproducible, minimizes bias, provides strong evidence for practice or policy decisions, identifies gaps in research.

Cons: Time-consuming and resource-intensive, requires expertise in methodology and statistics, limited by the quality of included studies.

Scoping Review

A scoping review is used to map the existing literature on a broad topic or question. It aims to identify the nature and extent of research evidence, rather than to synthesize findings to answer a specific question like a systematic review.

  • Identify the research question (broad).
  • Identify relevant studies (iterative search strategy).
  • Select studies based on inclusion criteria.
  • Chart the data (extract key information).
  • Collate, summarize, and report the results.
  • (Optional) Consult with stakeholders.

Pros: Useful for exploring complex or heterogeneous topics, can identify knowledge gaps, informs future research or systematic reviews, relatively flexible.

Cons: Does not typically assess study quality, findings are descriptive rather than evaluative, can be broad and less focused than systematic reviews.

Narrative Review

A narrative review provides a broad overview of a topic, often focusing on key studies or concepts. It is less structured and systematic than a systematic review and typically reflects the author's perspective and interpretation of the literature.

  • Broad in scope.
  • Selection of studies is often subjective.
  • Synthesis is qualitative and interpretive.
  • Focuses on summarizing and discussing existing knowledge.

Pros: Useful for introducing a topic, can provide historical context, relatively quick to complete, allows for author's insights and perspectives.

Cons: Prone to selection bias, not reproducible, findings can be subjective, limited in providing definitive evidence for specific questions.

Meta-Analysis

A meta-analysis is a statistical technique used to combine the results of multiple independent studies that address the same research question. It is often conducted as part of a systematic review to provide a single, pooled estimate of an effect.

  • Requires a systematic review as a foundation.
  • Extract quantitative data from included studies.
  • Assess heterogeneity between studies.
  • Apply statistical methods to pool results (e.g., fixed-effect or random-effects models).
  • Interpret pooled results and assess publication bias.

Pros: Increases statistical power, provides a more precise estimate of effect size, can resolve conflicting results from individual studies, identifies factors influencing results (subgroup analysis).

Cons: Requires homogeneous studies (or careful handling of heterogeneity), "garbage in, garbage out" principle applies (depends on quality of included studies), can be complex statistically, prone to publication bias.

Integrative Review

An integrative review is a method that synthesizes findings from diverse methodologies (e.g., quantitative, qualitative, mixed-methods) to develop a more comprehensive understanding of a phenomenon. It can include theoretical literature as well as empirical studies.

  • Problem identification.
  • Literature search.
  • Data evaluation.
  • Data analysis (synthesis).
  • Presentation of results.

Pros: Can provide a holistic view by combining different types of evidence, useful for complex topics or emerging areas, can inform theory development.

Cons: Can be challenging to synthesize diverse methodologies, potential for bias in study selection and synthesis, findings may be less definitive than systematic reviews focusing on quantitative data.
