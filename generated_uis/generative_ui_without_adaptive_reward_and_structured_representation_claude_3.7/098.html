Formal vs. Casual Japanese

Understanding Japanese Politeness Levels

Japanese language has distinct levels of politeness, primarily categorized into Formal (丁寧語 - Teineigo) and Casual (普通語 - Futsūgo). Choosing the correct level is crucial for effective communication and showing respect in different social contexts.

Formal Japanese is used in situations requiring politeness and respect, such as speaking to superiors, strangers, or in business settings.

Casual Japanese is used with close friends, family, or when speaking to oneself.

Key Characteristics

Formal Japanese (丁寧語 - Teineigo)

  • Uses polite verb endings like -masu (～ます)Used for verbs, e.g., 食べます (tabemasu - eat) and -desu (～です)Used for nouns and adjectives, e.g., 学生です (gakusei desu - I am a student).
  • Employs honorific prefixes like o- (お-)Used with native Japanese words, e.g., お金 (okane - money) and go- (ご-)Used with Sino-Japanese words, e.g., ご飯 (gohan - rice).
  • Often uses more complex sentence structures and vocabulary.
  • Sounds more reserved and respectful.

Casual Japanese (普通語 - Futsūgo)

  • Uses plain verb forms (dictionary form, -ru, -ta, etc.).
  • Omits particles or uses simpler ones.
  • May use contractions or slang.
  • Sounds more direct and familiar.

Example Comparisons

Let's look at some common phrases and how they differ in formal and casual speech. Click the play button to hear the pronunciation.

Formal

こんにちは。お元気ですか？

Konnichiwa. O-genki desu ka?

Hello. How are you?

Casual

やあ。元気？

Yaa. Genki?

Hey. How are you?

Formal

ありがとうございます。

Arigatō gozaimasu.

Thank you (very much).

Casual

ありがとう。

Arigatō.

Thanks.

Formal

はい、わかります。

Hai, wakarimasu.

Yes, I understand.

Casual

うん、わかる。

Un, wakaru.

Yeah, I get it.

Tip: When in Doubt, Use Formal!

If you are unsure which form to use, it's generally safer and more polite to use the formal form. Using casual Japanese in a formal situation can be considered rude.
