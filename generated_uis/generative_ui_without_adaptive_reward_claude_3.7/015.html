1. Crawling: Discovering the Web

Before Google can show search results, it needs to discover what content exists on the web.

Google's crawling process begins with a list of known web addresses from past crawls and sitemaps provided by website owners. Programs called crawlersAutomated programs that browse the web to discover and collect information about web pages. (also known as spiders or bots) visit these websites, much like you would by clicking on links.


The main crawler, called GooglebotGoogle's web crawler that discovers new and updated pages to add to the Google index., uses an algorithmic process to determine which sites to crawl, how often, and how many pages to fetch from each site. The crawling process follows these steps:


  1. Discovery: Googlebot finds pages through links from known pages, sitemaps, and submitted URLs.
  2. Fetching: The crawler requests the page from the server, similar to how a browser does.
  3. Processing: Googlebot renders the page and analyzes its content and links.
  4. Following: It follows links on the page to discover new URLs to crawl.

Website owners can control how Google crawls their site using the robots.txtA text file that tells search engine crawlers which pages or files the crawler can or can't request from your site. file and other directives. This helps Google crawl the most important content efficiently.

2. Indexing: Understanding and Storing Content

Once content is crawled, Google processes and analyzes it to understand what it's about.

Indexing is the process where Google analyzes and stores information about crawled pages. During indexing, Google:


  • Processes the content: Google analyzes the text, images, videos, and other media on the page.
  • Renders the page: Using a system similar to a browser, Google processes JavaScript and CSS to see the page as users would.
  • Understands the meaning: Using natural language processingA field of AI that gives computers the ability to understand text and spoken words similar to how humans can., Google determines what the page is about.
  • Catalogs information: The page is categorized and added to Google's massive index, ready to be retrieved when relevant to a search query.

Not all crawled pages are indexed. Google may choose not to index pages that are:


  • Duplicates of already-indexed content
  • Low quality or containing little value
  • Marked with a noindex tagAn HTML tag that tells search engines not to include a specific page in their index.
  • Blocked by robots.txt (though this alone doesn't prevent indexing)

Google's index is constantly updating as new pages are discovered, existing pages change, and outdated content is removed.

3. Ranking: Delivering Relevant Results

When you search, Google's algorithms work to find the most relevant and useful results from billions of indexed pages.

Ranking is where Google's algorithm truly shines. When you enter a search query, Google:


  1. Interprets your query: Understanding what you're looking for, including spelling corrections, synonyms, and search intent.
  2. Retrieves matching pages: Finding pages from the index that are related to your query.
  3. Ranks the results: Ordering them by relevance using hundreds of ranking factors.
  4. Returns the best results: Presenting them in a format that's most useful to you.

Google uses over 200 ranking factors in its algorithm. While the exact details are proprietary, some of the known key factors include:

📝

Content Quality

Google evaluates if content is comprehensive, accurate, and helpful to users. It looks for expertise, authoritativeness, and trustworthiness (E-A-T).

🔗

Backlinks

Links from other websites serve as "votes of confidence." The original PageRankGoogle's original algorithm that ranked pages based on the quantity and quality of links pointing to them. algorithm was built on this concept.

📱

Mobile-Friendliness

With most searches now coming from mobile devices, Google prioritizes sites that provide a good mobile experience.

⚡

Page Speed

Faster-loading pages provide a better user experience and may rank higher, especially on mobile searches.

🔒

Security

Google gives preference to secure websites (HTTPS over HTTP) to protect user data and privacy.

👥

User Experience

Metrics like Core Web VitalsA set of metrics that measure real-world user experience for loading performance, interactivity, and visual stability. help Google evaluate if users have a good experience on the page.

4. Spam Detection: Ensuring Quality Results

Google works hard to identify and filter out content that tries to manipulate rankings or deceive users.

To maintain the quality of search results, Google has sophisticated systems to detect attempts to manipulate rankings. These include:


  • Spam filtersAlgorithms designed to detect and penalize content that violates Google's Webmaster Guidelines.: Automated systems that identify content violating Google's guidelines.
  • Manual actions: Human reviewers can flag sites that don't comply with Google's quality guidelines.
  • Algorithm updates: Regular updates like PandaA Google algorithm update that targets low-quality content and content farms., PenguinA Google algorithm update that targets manipulative link building practices., and others target specific types of spam or low-quality content.

Common spam techniques that Google penalizes include:


  • Keyword stuffing (overusing keywords unnaturally)
  • Link schemes and buying links
  • Cloaking (showing different content to users versus search engines)
  • Hidden text or links
  • Doorway pages (pages created just for search engines)
  • Scraped or duplicate content

Google's spam detection systems are constantly evolving to stay ahead of new manipulation techniques, ensuring that users get high-quality results.

Glossary of Key Terms

Important concepts and terminology related to Google's search algorithm.

Algorithm
A complex computer program that looks for clues to give you back exactly what you want. Google's search algorithm uses over 200 factors to rank webpages.
BERT
Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers - a neural network-based technique for natural language processing that helps Google better understand the context of words in search queries.
Core Web Vitals
A set of metrics measuring user experience including loading performance (LCP), interactivity (FID), and visual stability (CLS).
Crawler
A program that visits websites and reads their pages to create entries for a search engine index. Google's main crawler is called Googlebot.
E-A-T
Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness - principles Google uses to assess content quality, especially for YMYL (Your Money Your Life) topics.
Index
Google's index is like a library where it stores all the webpages it knows about. When you search, Google looks through the index for relevant information.
MUM
Multitask Unified Model - an AI technology 1000 times more powerful than BERT, helping Google understand complex queries and multimodal content.
PageRank
Google's original ranking algorithm that evaluates the quantity and quality of links to a webpage to determine its importance.
RankBrain
A machine learning component of Google's algorithm that helps process and understand search queries, especially new ones never seen before.
Search Intent
The goal behind a user's search query - whether they're looking for information, trying to make a purchase, or navigating to a specific site.
