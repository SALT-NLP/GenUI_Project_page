💬 Qualitative Data Analysis

Interviewer: Can you tell me about your daily social media routine?
Participant: I usually check Instagram first thing in the morning, even before I get out of bed. Then throughout the day, I'm constantly on TikTok whenever I have a free moment. I probably spend about 6-7 hours total on social media every day.
Interviewer: How does using social media make you feel?
Participant: Sometimes I feel anxious if I haven't checked my notifications for a while. Like I'm missing out on something important. I get stressed when I see people with perfect lives and I compare myself to them. But I also feel connected to my friends, which is nice. It's complicated.
Interviewer: Have you noticed any changes in your mood related to social media use?
Participant: Definitely. If I spend too many hours scrolling, I start to feel down about myself. It's like this weird cycle where I feel bad, so I scroll more to distract myself, but then I end up feeling worse. I've tried taking breaks sometimes, and I notice I feel more present and less anxious.
Interviewer: What aspects of social media do you think affect your wellbeing the most?
Participant: Definitely the comparison part. Seeing people with seemingly perfect bodies, relationships, and lives makes me feel inadequate. But the positive interactions with friends and finding communities of people with similar interests has been really helpful for me. I think it's about balance and being mindful of how different content affects me.

Synthesis Narrative

The integration of quantitative and qualitative findings reveals several key insights about the psychological effects of social media use among teenagers:

1. Usage Patterns and Anxiety: The quantitative data shows a moderate positive correlation (r=0.42) between daily social media usage and anxiety scores. This statistical relationship is illuminated by qualitative accounts where participants described feelings of anxiety when unable to check notifications and a compulsive need to stay connected. The interview data adds depth by revealing the cyclical nature of this relationship - anxiety leads to increased usage, which then exacerbates anxiety.

2. Social Comparison Mechanisms: While the survey data indicated widespread social comparison behaviors (68% reporting frequent comparison), the qualitative data clarifies the specific nature of these comparisons (physical appearance, social relationships, lifestyle) and the emotional impact they have. This triangulation strengthens the conclusion that social comparison is a key mediating factor between social media use and psychological well-being.

3. Platform-Specific Effects: Both data sources suggest differential impacts based on platform, with image-focused platforms like Instagram showing stronger associations with negative outcomes compared to other platforms. The qualitative data provides context for why these differences exist, pointing to the content types and interaction patterns unique to each platform.
