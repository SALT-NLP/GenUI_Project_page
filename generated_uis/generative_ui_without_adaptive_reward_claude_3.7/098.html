Formal vs Casual Japanese

Understanding the key differences and when to use each style

Introduction to Japanese Speech Styles

Japanese is unique in how clearly it distinguishes between formal and casual speech. Unlike English, where formality is often expressed through word choice, Japanese has distinct grammatical patterns, verb endings, and vocabulary that change depending on the level of formality required.

This distinction is crucial for anyone learning Japanese, as using the wrong speech style can lead to awkward social situations or even be considered rude in certain contexts.

Formal Japanese (敬語 - Keigo)

Formal Japanese, often called 敬語Keigo - honorific language, is used in professional settings, with strangers, superiors, customers, or in any situation requiring politeness and respect.

Key Characteristics:

  • Verb endings with -ます (-masu) and -です (-desu)
  • Honorific prefixes (お/ご - o/go)
  • More complete sentences
  • Polite vocabulary choices
  • Less use of contractions

Casual Japanese (タメ語 - Tamego)

Casual Japanese, sometimes called タメ語Tamego - plain/familiar language, is used with friends, family, close colleagues, and in relaxed social situations.

Key Characteristics:

  • Plain form verb endings (no -ます/-です)
  • Shortened sentences and phrases
  • Contractions and slang
  • More direct expressions
  • Casual vocabulary choices

When to Use Each Style

Use Formal Japanese (敬語) When:

  • Speaking to strangers
  • In business or professional settings
  • When meeting someone for the first time
  • Speaking to customers or clients
  • Speaking to teachers, professors, or superiors
  • In formal written communication
  • During job interviews or formal events

Use Casual Japanese (タメ語) When:

  • Speaking with close friends
  • Talking to family members
  • In casual social gatherings
  • Speaking with colleagues of the same rank (after establishing rapport)
  • In informal written communication (text messages, social media)
  • Speaking to children
  • In casual blog posts or personal writing

Grammar Differences

Here are some examples showing how basic grammar changes between formal and casual Japanese:

Verb Conjugation - "To eat"

Formal: 食べます (tabemasu)

Casual: 食べる (taberu)

Negative Form - "Don't eat"

Formal: 食べません (tabemasen)

Casual: 食べない (tabenai)

Past Tense - "Ate"

Formal: 食べました (tabemashita)

Casual: 食べた (tabeta)

Negative Past - "Didn't eat"

Formal: 食べませんでした (tabemasen deshita)

Casual: 食べなかった (tabenakatta)

Tips for Navigating Formality in Japanese

1
Default to Formal

When in doubt, it's always safer to use formal Japanese. You can gradually shift to casual speech as you become more familiar with someone.

2
Watch for Social Cues

Pay attention to how others speak to you. If they use casual language, it may be an invitation for you to do the same.

3
Be Careful with Mixing Styles

Mixing formal and casual speech in the same sentence can sound unnatural. Try to maintain consistency in your speech style.

4
Learn Set Phrases

Memorize common phrases in both formal and casual forms so you can switch between them appropriately.

5
Practice Active Listening

Pay attention to how native speakers adjust their language based on social context. This will help you develop a natural feel for when to use each style.

Common Phrases Comparison

"Hello"

Formal: こんにちは (Konnichiwa)

Casual: やあ (Yaa) / ヤッホー (Yahhoo)

"Thank you"

Formal: ありがとうございます (Arigatou gozaimasu)

Casual: ありがとう (Arigatou) / サンキュー (Sankyuu)

"Sorry"

Formal: すみません (Sumimasen) / 申し訳ありません (Moushiwake arimasen)

Casual: ごめん (Gomen) / ごめんね (Gomen ne)

"Please"

Formal: お願いします (Onegai shimasu)

Casual: お願い (Onegai) / 頼む (Tanomu)
